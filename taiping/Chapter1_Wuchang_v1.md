# Return to Life: A Boy's War in the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom

> **Editorial Note**
>
> This is a first-person English adaptation of 《复生录》(Records of Return to Life), the memoir of Chen Sibo (陈思伯), who at age sixteen was conscripted into the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom army in 1852 and survived four years of war before escaping home.
>
> The primary source for this adaptation is the author's modern Chinese rewrite (published on Zhihu, 350,000+ reads), which expands the original classical Chinese text with battlefield detail, material culture, and narrative pacing suited to contemporary readers. The original 覆生录 by Chen Sibo serves as a secondary source for voice calibration and factual correction.
>
> This is not a translation. It is a re-creation — the same story, retold for English-speaking readers in the tradition of *All Quiet on the Western Front*: immersive, sensory, and anchored in the perspective of an ordinary soldier.
>
> — snail0000haaa

## Chapter 1 — Wuchang

> He rode the yellow crane away. He did not return.
> The tower remains. The clouds have drifted here a thousand years.
>
> Hanyang's trees are clear across the water.
> The grass grows wild on Parrot Island.
>
> Dusk. Where is home?
> Mist on the river. A heart that cannot rest.
>
> — Cui Hao (704–754), *Yellow Crane Tower*

That poem was written four hundred years before I was born, about a tower that stood on Snake Hill, above the city where I spent the worst morning of my life. The poet had been exiled. He stood on that hill and looked out over the Yangtze and could not find his way home. I understand him now in a way I could not when I was sixteen, sitting in my uncle's house on Chaoyang Lane, reciting his verses for the civil service exams, believing that the world was arranged in a certain order and that order would hold.

It did not hold.

---

The Taiping came from the south.

They had begun in the far mountains of Guangxi, a sect of poverty-stricken farmers and charcoal burners led by a man who claimed to be the younger brother of Jesus Christ. By the winter of 1852, they had swept through two provinces and were moving north along the Yangtze like a flood that had found its channel.

I did not see most of this. I heard it — fragments from travelers, from officials passing through Wuchang, from the frightened talk of adults who stopped speaking whenever children entered the room. What I knew was this: the rebels had taken Hanyang first, then Hankou, and now they sat across the river from us, building something I had never seen before.

They built floating bridges across the Yangtze.

Two of them. The upper bridge ran from Nianyutao to the east gate of Hanyang; the lower one stretched from the Caobu Gate to the Longwang Temple in Hankou. Each bridge was wide enough for three men to walk abreast. They had sunk iron anchors into the riverbed to hold the boats in place, but the Yangtze does not hold still for anyone. Even on windless days, the bridges rose and fell with the current, swaying, creaking. The timorous could not cross them.

I watched from the city walls and wondered: who would build a road on water?

They had also done something I could not understand at the time, though later it would strike me as perfectly characteristic. They had taken five hundred gilded arhats from the Guiyuan Temple — five hundred lacquered disciples of Buddha, each one a work of devotion — and set them along the ramparts of Hanyang to stand guard. The worshippers of the Heavenly Father, who had smashed every idol from Guangxi to Hubei, had pressed Buddha's own disciples into service. In the Kingdom of Peace, even the gods were conscripted.

I did not know then that the arhats were a feint. The bridges were the real work. The Taiping did not need Buddha to guard Hanyang. They needed him to keep our eyes on the walls while they dug under ours.

---

They had come up from Hunan, where they had recruited miners — men thrown out of work when the silver veins ran dry, men who knew how to carve passages through rock and earth. The Taiping gave them a new name and a new uniform and a new god, and in return, the miners gave them the art of the tunnel.

This was not a new craft. Siege mining had been practiced in China since the Warring States, two thousand years before. But the Taiping had made it their signature. Every major city they took had fallen the same way: a passage dug beneath the walls, packed with black powder, and lit. Then the wall came down, and they came through the gap.

There were ways to detect a mine. You dug pits near the base of the wall and sank great clay vats into them. Then you seated blind men inside the vats. A blind man's ears compensate for what his eyes cannot do. He can hear the scratch of picks underground, the direction from which the digging comes. Once you know where the tunnel is, you counter-mine: dig your own passage above it, flood it with water, and wait for the powder to spoil in the damp.

Our governor was a man named Chang Dacun. He had just arrived in Hubei when the siege began. Reports came to him: the rebels are digging. Sappers have been heard beneath the walls near the Wenchang Gate. He did not believe the reports. Or perhaps — as some said later — there was no money left to pay the men who would have done the counter-mining. Either way, the vats stayed empty. The blind men were never seated.

On the fourth day of the twelfth month, at dawn, three tunnels went off at once.

---

I remember the sound.

Not the blast itself — that was too large for hearing, more a pressure in the chest, as if the air had been punched out of the room. What I remember is the silence after, a silence so complete it seemed the whole city had drawn a single breath and held it. Then the screaming started, and the drums, and the trumpets from the rebel camps across the river, and somewhere in between, the sound of stone crashing down into the moat.

Sixty meters of wall collapsed at the Wenchang Gate. Through the dust and the noise they came.

The garrison fought. I do not know how long. By afternoon the Taiping had opened the Caobu Gate and their boats were in the city, and the fighting was over. Some of the imperial troops escaped through the Binyang Gate on the east side, those who could run. Those who could not — and there were many — died where they stood.

The worst of it was the women.

They threw themselves into every pond and well in the city. The ponds filled with bodies until the water was gone and the women who came after had nothing left to drown in. The wells clogged with them. Days later, even the Taiping could not bear it. They began pulling survivors out of the water and the wells, hauling these half-drowned women onto the banks and telling them to live.

I did not see this with my own eyes. I was in my uncle's house on Chaoyang Lane, at the foot of Snake Hill, and what I knew came to me in fragments — the sound of the blast, the smoke rising from the south wall, the screaming that went on for hours, and then the silence that was worse.

---

My uncle's name was Zhi Chen. I had been living in his house for some time. My father had died less than a year before, and I was still in mourning — the white clothes, the uncut hair, the ritual prohibitions that a son observes for the parent he has lost. My mother was in Wuchang too, in another part of the city, with my younger brother. My cousin Xizhi, Uncle's son, was also in the house.

When the city fell, Uncle told Xizhi and me to hide in the small library upstairs, in the rear hall. "Do not come down," he said. "No matter what you hear."

On the second day, they came through the door.

I heard the wood splinter. I heard boots on the stairs. I heard my aunt's voice, and then the voices of strangers — loud, commanding, speaking in the dialects of Guangxi and Hunan. They were searching the houses, room by room.

They found the chests.

The wooden chests stacked under the bedding in the rear room. They pried them open and pulled out what was inside: Flying Tiger banners. Military signal flags — markers used by garrison troops to identify their camps and rally their columns. My father had once served in Hunan. The banners were his, stored here after his death, forgotten in a trunk under a bed. But to the men who held them up now — red silk, stained with old lacquer, fringed with gold thread — they were proof. This household belonged to an officer of the Qing garrison. Therefore, the man of the house was an officer. Therefore, he would die.

They announced they would take my uncle to their commander and execute him on the spot.

---

From the loft, I heard the wailing.

The women of the house were screaming — my aunt, the servants, the neighbors who had taken shelter with us. Children were crying. And beneath it all, the sound of my uncle's voice, calm but thin, trying to reason with men who had already decided.

Xizhi stood in the corner of the loft. His face was blank. His mouth was open but nothing came out. His hands hung at his sides. I do not blame him. He was a young man who had never seen a blade drawn in anger, and now men with blades were in his house, and they were about to kill his father. There is a point at which the mind simply stops. It is not cowardice. It is something more mechanical than that — a fuse blown, a circuit broken.

I was sixteen. My father had been dead eleven months. I was wearing the white clothes of mourning, and my hair was uncut, and there were tablets on the ancestral altar that I was supposed to bow to every morning. I did not think about any of this. I did not think at all.

Something hot rose from my chest to my skull. And before I could stop myself, I was shouting down from the loft:

"Bring a ladder!"

---

Every face in the room turned upward.

The rebels. The women. The children crouching against the walls. They all looked up at me, a boy in white mourning clothes, standing at the edge of the loft, gripping the railing.

A ladder came. I do not remember who brought it. I climbed down, and I stood in front of them.

"My uncle is a merchant. He has never held rank. Those banners belonged to my father, who once served in Hunan. They are his property, not my uncle's. Whatever you charge him with — it has nothing to do with him."

I heard my own voice. It did not sound like mine. It was steady.

"Let him go. If someone must die for those banners, I will take his place."

I do not know what I expected. A blade, most likely. A blow. The room was very quiet. The men looked at one another. Something passed between them — not pity, not mercy, but a kind of rough recognition. One of them said something to another in a dialect I could not follow. Then the leader turned back to me.

He offered me a red headband.

The mark of the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom. If I put it on, he said, they would post a notice of protection on our door. No soldier would touch the household. The deal was simple: my life for my family's safety.

---

I carried my father's spirit tablet to the central hall.

The tablet was a small wooden plaque with his name written on it in gold — the physical anchor of his spirit in this world, the thing I had been bowing to every morning since his death. I placed it on the ancestral altar alongside the tablets of his father, and his father's father, and all the Chen ancestors stretching back to the founding of the lineage.

I knelt. I bowed until my forehead touched the floor.

I said nothing aloud. What could I say? That I was sorry? That I had no choice? Both were true, and neither mattered. I was about to remove the white clothes of mourning. I was about to put on the red headband of the Heavenly Kingdom — the kingdom that had just killed thousands of my city's people, the kingdom that my father, had he lived, would have fought against. I was about to commit an act that, by every law of my ancestors and every teaching of my upbringing, was unforgivable.

I asked forgiveness anyway. Then I stood up, and I took off the white clothes, and I put on the red headband.

I went to the family shrine and bowed to every ancestor on the register. Then I turned to the assembled elders — my uncle, my aunts, the seniors of the household — and knelt once more.

No one spoke. There was nothing left to say.

My uncle stopped me at the door. He put his hand on my shoulder and looked at me for a long time. Then he said: "From now on, your name is Sibo."

思伯. *Si* — to remember. *Bo* — the uncle's branch of the family. The name meant: remember to come back.

I told my younger brother to take care of our mother. I did not look back after that. I walked out into the street, and the red headband on my forehead drew a line through my life that I would not cross again for four years.

The Long-Haired men were everywhere. The streets of Wuchang smelled of smoke and something else — a sweetness I did not recognize then but would come to know well in the years ahead: the smell of blood in large quantities, drying in the winter air.

I followed the man who had given me the red headband. He took me to a camp on the outskirts of the city, where I was assigned to a company under a small commander named Zheng Apei. Zheng looked me over and saw what I was: a bookish boy who could barely hold a blade. He put me to work managing documents — the written orders, the daily reports, the sacred texts that every Taiping soldier was required to memorize.

There were rules. Many rules. The "Ten Heavenly Commandments." The "Taiping Regulations." The "Twenty Military Articles." And there were punishments: death for rape, death for killing a surrendered enemy, death for burning a civilian's house without cause. In those early days, the Taiping kept their discipline. This was not the rabble the imperial officials described in their reports. It was an army, organized and stern, with a god who watched and a code that was enforced.

But the code had a particular commandment that would shape everything that came after. In the Heavenly Kingdom, men and women lived separately. Even husbands and wives were forbidden to meet. To be caught in private conversation with a member of the opposite sex was death for both. It was a law that ran against every instinct of the human heart, and it would produce, in time, behaviors that the law itself could not contain.

I did not know any of this yet. I was sixteen, wearing a red headband, standing in a camp full of strangers, trying to remember the new name my uncle had given me.

*Sibo. Remember to come back.*

I did not know it would take four years.

## Editorial Notes

**Assumptions made:**
- The Yellow Crane Tower poem is used as an epigraph and thematic frame. I translated it rather than using any existing translation, to match the narrative voice.
- The tunnel warfare and counter-mining techniques are narrated through "I" as knowledge the narrator possessed at the time or acquired later, not as a break in the narrative.
- The "blind men in vats" technique is presented as something "I" knew about but the governor failed to act on, maintaining narrative flow.
- The "arhats on the walls" detail from the user's version is preserved and given additional commentary ("even the gods were conscripted") that fits the first-person reflective tone.
- The "Flying Tiger banners" are given space as a physical object in the narrative — the reader sees them being pulled from a trunk, sees their texture and color — because their narrative function is enormous.
- The farewell scene expands the original's "伯命改名思伯" from a single statement into a full scene with physical actions.

**Evidence or sourcing gaps:**
- The exact position of the Chen family house on Chaoyang Lane relative to the city wall is inferred, not confirmed.
- Whether the narrator could actually see the floating bridges from the city walls is assumed but not verified.
- The timing of the women's suicides (same day as the fall vs. days later) differs between the original and the user's version; I used the user's version.

**Tone or credibility risks:**
- The line "even the gods were conscripted" may read as too polished for a narrator who is supposed to be recounting events simply. If it feels like the author's voice intruding, it can be cut.
- The transition from the city's fall to the search of the Chen household may feel abrupt. A connecting passage about the passage of time (one day, two days) could help.
